Carolyn Abott is an Associate Professor of Political Science at Baruch College–City University of New York. She studies American political institutions, with a focus on state and local governance, public finance, and interest group politics.

Her book *The Politics of Public Pensions: Parties, State Governments, and Unions* (Columbia University Press, 2025) examines how partisan competition and organized labor shape public sector pension policy across the American states. The book argues that state governments occupy a distinctive middle ground in the American federal system—more insulated from national party polarization than Congress, yet more institutionally capable than most local governments—and that this position has profound consequences for pension funding, fiscal transparency, and the distribution of public sector benefits.

Her current book project, *The Uncontested City: Interest Groups, Nonprofits, and Democratic Inequality in One-Party America*, examines how organized interests—including nonprofits, neighborhood associations, and community boards—shape local governance and resource allocation in cities where electoral competition is limited.

Her broader research program examines how fiscal institutions shape democratic accountability and distributive outcomes at the state and local level. This includes work on municipal fiscal distress and bankruptcy—providing some of the first systematic causal evidence of bankruptcy's fiscal and service delivery consequences—as well as research on how voters respond to fiscal signals and hold elected officials accountable for government financial health. Her work on education finance integrates questions of electoral design with resource allocation, offering causal evidence that operational school spending raises student achievement, particularly for Black, Latino, and low-income students in under-resourced districts, and that converting from at-large to district elections increases minority representation. Her research on electoral rules and minority representation engages directly with questions arising in redistricting and voting rights litigation, including the causal effects of at-large versus district elections on the descriptive representation of Latino and other minority communities. She has also examined how federal tax policy changes, including the SALT deduction cap, affect state and local public finance.

Before joining Baruch College, she was an Assistant Professor in the Department of Government and Politics at St. John's University (2018–2021) and a Postdoctoral Research Fellow at the John Glenn College of Public Affairs and the Department of Political Science at The Ohio State University (2016–2018). She holds a Ph.D. in Politics and Social Policy from Princeton University (2016) and a B.A. in Economics and Political Science, with High Honors, from Swarthmore College (2008). Prior to graduate school she worked as an Assistant Economist at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York.
